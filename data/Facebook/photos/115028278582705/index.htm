Dandi MengAs Tom has noted, the books behind the subject of the photograph hold special significance, but I must suggest that we look to one book in particular, the one situated directly behind the subject's head, entitled Hall of Mirrors. Notice how precariously close the subject's head comes to blocking the title. Here, we get our first clue that the book's title is no mere coincidence--the photographer is clearly alluding to the unreliability of representation through the book's precarious positioning and the allusion to the notoriously mystifying house of mirrors (Key&Peele, 2014), which, despite its initial appearance as an accurate reflection of reality, often turns out to deceive us and bring out our darkest tendencies. However, to truly grasp the revolutionary aesthetic of this piece, I argue that we must examine more closely the trajectory of the photographer's entire oeuvre, as well as engage with the full history of portraiture as embodied by his works. In looking back at entire collection of the photographer's profile pictures, we must note the preponderance of non-human animals who have taken his place in the frame. Whether the tone of this obvious displacement is ironic, ha-ha-funny, or serious (perhaps a strident critique of anthropocentrism?) is open for debate, but we must not lose sight of the photographer's willingness to interrogate his own centrality to his profile. At the same time however, I feel compelled to point out that the photographer has long been experimenting with the form of the portrait. We see him come back to iterations of this poser over and over again in his vast body of work, each time adjusting the angle at which his head is tilted, almost as if for him, looking for his "good side" is not a binaristic matter, but one which requires infinite degrees of precision, which must be endlessly refined. This is an essentially utopian stance on the future of portraiture, which we must applaud along with the posthumanist gestures detailed above.
